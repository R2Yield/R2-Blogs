# R2 Macro Structure Notes | Vol. 10
## Oil Pullback, Tactical Relief, Structural Pressure Still Remains

Over the past 72 hours, global markets experienced one of the sharpest shifts in macro expectations this year.

Crude oil surged violently on fears of a prolonged Middle East escalation, only to reverse just as fast after a change in political signaling from Washington. Equities, crypto, and broader risk assets all responded in sync. Bitcoin and Ethereum stabilized after testing lower support, while the S&P 500 found temporary relief as energy panic faded.

At first glance, this looks like a clean easing of macro pressure. But under the surface, the picture is more nuanced.

What the market is pricing right now is not a return to safety. It is a repricing from the most extreme scenario.

That distinction matters.

---

## 1. The market was not trading fundamentals. It was trading tail risk.

The violent move in oil was never just about current supply disruption. It was about the possibility that the conflict could evolve into a broader and longer war, one that would pull the Strait of Hormuz into sustained disruption and reprice global inflation expectations all at once.

That fear drove a sharp risk-off move across markets. Oil spiked, equity downside accelerated, and crypto remained under pressure as investors began to price in a world where higher energy costs, tighter financial conditions, and weaker growth would hit simultaneously.

Then the tone changed.

Trump’s comments over the past day shifted the market narrative materially. His language suggested that, from his perspective, the campaign may remain limited in duration rather than evolving into an open-ended regional war. At the same time, coordinated discussions around strategic petroleum reserve releases added another layer of relief to the supply outlook.

This combination broke the market’s most extreme oil narrative. Crude collapsed from panic highs, and the unwind in energy stress gave breathing room to equities and crypto.

But again, it is important to frame this correctly: markets are not pricing peace. Markets are pricing a lower probability of the worst-case outcome.

## 2. Bitcoin and Ethereum are stabilizing, but neither has fully escaped the macro shadow.

On the crypto side, both BTC and ETH improved structurally compared with the previous session.

Bitcoin reclaimed its daily capital flow support zone, turning what had been a contested resistance area back into support. Short-term momentum recovered, and the internal long-short balance shifted back toward a slight bullish edge. Ethereum showed a similar pattern. Despite recent downside pressure, it also pushed back above its short-term flow zone, with positioning metrics now favoring a modest rebound bias.

This is meaningful, but it should not be overstated.

From a market structure perspective, both assets are still trading inside a broader stabilization range rather than a confirmed new uptrend. In other words, this is a tactical improvement, not yet a full trend reversal.

For BTC, the market has shown that buyers are still willing to defend lower support zones aggressively. For ETH, sub-$2,000 weakness continues to attract positioning interest, especially as valuation metrics remain depressed relative to recent history. But in both cases, upside remains capped unless macro conditions continue to improve.

Crypto is no longer trading as an isolated asset class. Between ETF flows, the growing role of stablecoins in Treasury demand, and increasing institutional participation, BTC and ETH now sit much closer to the center of the global macro system than they did in prior cycles.

That means macro relief can help them bounce quickly. It also means macro deterioration can still drag them lower even when internal crypto positioning looks constructive.

## 3. The Fed still has no clean path.

One of the most important tensions in the market right now is that the growth data is weakening, but the Federal Reserve still cannot respond freely.

Recent US data continues to show softness. Labor conditions are no longer clearly resilient, and consumption data has failed to show convincing reacceleration. In a normal environment, that would have been enough to push the market harder toward rate cuts.

But this is not a normal environment.

As long as oil remains politically sensitive and inflation risks can be reignited by geopolitical shocks, the Fed remains constrained. The market may want easier policy, but policymakers still have to worry that a fresh energy spike would reverse inflation progress very quickly.

This is why recent weak economic data has not translated into a clean bullish macro pivot.

Growth is slowing, but policy is not yet ready to save the market. That leaves risk assets in an unstable middle ground: bad enough to create stress, but not bad enough to guarantee immediate monetary relief.

## 4. Equities may have found a short-term floor, but the larger valuation problem remains unresolved.

The S&P 500 reacted exactly as one would expect in this environment. It sold off aggressively when oil panic peaked, then found support once the geopolitical narrative softened and crude rolled over.

That short-term rebound makes sense. But it does not eliminate the broader issue.

US equities are still dealing with stretched valuations, high rates, and a macro backdrop that is becoming more fragile. Credit concerns have not disappeared. Private market redemption pressure has not disappeared. And if real yields remain elevated while earnings expectations begin to soften, equity downside will likely remain a recurring theme rather than a one-off scare.

In that sense, what we are seeing now may be better understood as a reprieve, not a resolution.

## 5. What matters next is not rhetoric alone, but duration.

The next move in markets will depend less on headlines themselves and more on whether this conflict actually shortens in duration.

If the current military cycle fades and the market becomes convinced that the operation remains temporary, oil can continue retracing, the dollar can soften, and risk assets can extend their rebound. Under that scenario, BTC and ETH have room to continue stabilizing and potentially push into a stronger relief rally.

But if the situation drags on, or if there is another escalation targeting energy infrastructure or leadership decapitation attempts, then the market will quickly rebuild the same tail-risk premium it just removed.

That would mean higher oil again, tighter financial conditions again, and renewed pressure across equities and crypto.

The market is currently trading on the belief that time is limited. If time expands, pricing will have to adjust again.

## 6. Our view

At R2, we continue to see this phase as one of tactical relief inside a still-fragile macro structure.

The oil collapse was significant, but it was primarily driven by a reversal in expectations, not by the full removal of geopolitical risk. Bitcoin and Ethereum are behaving better in the short term, but both remain highly sensitive to broader macro conditions. US growth is weakening, but policy flexibility remains constrained. Equities have bounced, yet valuation and credit stress have not been fully resolved.

In other words, the market has stepped back from the edge, but it has not fully walked away from the cliff.

For risk assets, this creates a window where rebounds can be sharp and tradable, but sustainability still depends on macro follow-through. For on-chain capital allocators, it reinforces the importance of distinguishing between temporary volatility relief and genuine structural improvement.

That distinction is exactly where disciplined asset management matters most.
