# R2 Macro Structure Notes | Vol. 15

## Inflation Is No Longer Falling Cleanly

The macro backdrop is becoming more difficult again, not because the market lacks optimism, but because the underlying pressures have not truly gone away. The latest U.S. inflation data shows that the disinflation story is no longer moving in a smooth direction. March CPI came in meaningfully above the prior reading, with energy acting as the main driver, while core inflation also edged higher. That matters because it suggests the oil shock is not remaining isolated inside one category. It is beginning to feed into the broader economy, and once that transmission starts, it tends to be slower and more stubborn than the market first expects.

This is the key point many risk markets still underestimate. Rising oil is not simply a sector-specific positive for U.S. energy producers. It is also a cost shock for the wider system. Higher crude eventually means more pressure on transport, services, input costs, and consumer spending. Even when the first move appears concentrated in energy, the second-order effect is often what matters most for macro. That is why inflation can stay sticky even when the initial trigger seems narrow.

### The Fed Still Has Little Room to Pivot

The PPI print offered some short-term relief because it came in below expectations, reducing immediate fears of another rate hike. But the broader message is still cautious. Producer inflation remains high, and the move higher from the prior reading suggests that cost pressure has not disappeared. It may simply be getting delayed or softened by inventory management, pricing decisions, and corporate buffers rather than being resolved at the source.

That leaves the Federal Reserve in a familiar but uncomfortable position. The bar for renewed tightening remains high, yet the bar for easing is also difficult to clear. Inflation is still too vulnerable to energy, and the persistence of price pressure means policymakers are likely to remain in wait-and-see mode. In practical terms, this means the market is not facing an immediate hawkish shock, but it is also not receiving the kind of clean policy support that would justify a full risk-on regime. It is a pause, not a pivot.

### Oil Remains the Most Important Macro Variable

The energy market is doing more than influencing inflation expectations. It is shaping the entire policy and risk environment. Oil remains highly sensitive to developments in the Middle East, and that sensitivity is what gives the current macro setup its fragility. A temporary easing in tensions can quickly pull crude lower and calm markets. A renewed disruption can do the opposite just as quickly.

That is why the current decline in oil should be interpreted carefully. It reflects a short-term improvement in geopolitical tone, not necessarily a durable return to stability. As long as the region remains unresolved, oil cannot be treated as a settled variable. And as long as oil is unsettled, inflation remains vulnerable, the Fed remains constrained, and broader markets remain exposed to fast repricing.

### The Middle East Is in a Pause, Not a Resolution

Recent negotiations have created a temporary buffer period, and markets are treating that as a source of relief. In the short run, that reaction makes sense. A pause in escalation lowers the immediate risk premium, softens demand for the dollar, supports equities, and improves appetite for risk assets more broadly. But the structure of the current diplomacy still looks fragile. The core disputes are not fully resolved, the guarantors are not especially strong, and the durability of any understanding remains uncertain.

That means the current environment should be viewed as a tactical calm rather than a strategic reset. The market has been given room to breathe, but not enough evidence to assume that the geopolitical problem is solved. If talks weaken, if external actors re-enter more aggressively, or if the temporary ceasefire loses credibility, the macro premium can return very quickly. That would immediately raise pressure on oil, inflation, and risk assets at the same time.

### The Dollar Has Softened, but Only Conditionally

The U.S. dollar has weakened as the geopolitical backdrop has become less acute, and that has been helpful for non-dollar assets. A softer dollar naturally supports global liquidity conditions and gives risk markets some room to recover. But this softness should not be mistaken for a new long-term trend. It depends on the assumption that tensions continue to ease and that inflation does not re-accelerate in a way that reintroduces policy stress.

In other words, dollar weakness is currently a symptom of temporary calm, not of a fully restored macro environment. If oil rises again or inflation reasserts itself in the next round of data, the dollar can firm quickly. That would tighten financial conditions again and reduce the space for the current relief rally to continue.

### Equities Are Rallying, but the Move Still Looks Tactical

The rebound in U.S. equities has been strong, but the interpretation should remain disciplined. Part of the move reflects genuine relief after a period of elevated geopolitical fear. Part reflects capital re-entering after standing aside. And part likely reflects an aggressive repricing of short-term sentiment rather than a definitive change in the macro foundation.

That distinction matters because the underlying vulnerabilities have not disappeared. Equity markets are still dealing with high valuations, still exposed to inflation persistence, and still dependent on a geopolitical backdrop that has only stabilized temporarily. This creates a market that can rally hard in the short term, but remains vulnerable to reversal if one of the major macro variables turns again. The current move is better understood as a tactical rebound than as confirmation of a durable new uptrend.

### Crypto Is Increasingly a Macro Asset Class

Even without focusing on BTC and ETH specifically, one macro shift deserves attention: digital assets are now far more integrated with the broader U.S. financial system than they were in prior cycles. ETFs, stablecoin growth, and the increasing connection between crypto liquidity and Treasury demand have made macro variables much more important to crypto pricing. That means inflation, policy, yields, oil, and geopolitics now matter far more directly to digital assets than many market participants still assume.

This does not mean crypto loses its idiosyncratic cycles. It means those cycles are now operating inside a larger macro framework. The implication is clear: investors can no longer analyze digital assets purely through internal industry narratives. U.S. macro policy and global risk conditions now carry much more weight in shaping their medium- and long-term path.

### The Market Is Trading Relief, Not Certainty

The broad picture is straightforward. Markets are benefiting from a temporary easing in pressure. Inflation is not accelerating uncontrollably, but it is no longer falling cleanly. The Fed is not turning aggressively hawkish, but it also is not ready to become supportive. Oil has come off its highs, but remains politically unstable. Geopolitical tensions have eased, but not been resolved. The dollar has softened, but only because the market believes the worst-case scenario has been delayed.

That is not a full macro reset. It is a relief phase inside an unresolved environment.

### Bottom Line

The correct macro lens remains one of caution and selectivity. The recent recovery in risk sentiment is real, but it rests on fragile assumptions: that energy pressure does not re-intensify, that inflation does not become more broadly sticky, and that the current diplomatic window in the Middle East does not break down. Until those assumptions are tested more fully, it is better to think in terms of tactical opportunities rather than broad structural confirmation.

This remains a market defined by oil, inflation persistence, policy constraint, and geopolitical uncertainty. And for now, that means macro is still in charge.
