## Introduction

The key issue in today’s market is no longer price itself, but the variables that are driving price.

For a period of time, markets appeared to stabilize under a relatively clear framework. Inflation was moderating, the rate cycle was approaching its peak, and risk assets were gradually recovering. However, recent developments have shown that this perceived stability was largely temporary. What appeared to be equilibrium was in fact a short-lived balance between competing forces.

Markets are now transitioning into a different regime, where price action is increasingly shaped by the interaction between macroeconomic conditions and geopolitical developments, rather than by liquidity alone.

## Macro as the Primary Driver

The dominant role of liquidity is fading, and macro variables are regaining control over market direction.

Interest rate expectations remain uncertain, and the path forward is no longer clearly defined. Inflation has moderated but not fully normalized, while real rates remain relatively elevated. At the same time, fiscal pressures continue to build, adding another layer of complexity to policy expectations.

As a result, markets no longer have a single anchor. In previous phases, expectations around rate cuts were sufficient to guide positioning. Today, that anchor is weaker, and multiple variables are interacting simultaneously. This has made market behavior more sensitive to changes in expectations rather than to confirmed data.

## The Expanding Role of Geopolitics

Geopolitical developments are now exerting a more immediate and direct influence on market behavior.

Recent price movements have been closely linked to developments in the Middle East. Changes in expectations around escalation or de-escalation have had a rapid impact on energy prices, which in turn has influenced broader risk sentiment.

The nature of this influence is asymmetric. De-escalation tends to produce short-term stabilization or relief rallies, while escalation carries the potential to trigger more sustained and structural repricing.

Markets are therefore increasingly trading expectations rather than outcomes. When expectations shift, price adjustments can be both rapid and significant. This creates an environment in which stability is fragile and easily disrupted.

## Policy Signaling and Market Interpretation

Another notable feature of the current environment is the increasing influence of policy signaling.

Markets have shown a growing sensitivity to communication, including public statements and forward guidance. In some cases, these signals have had a stronger short-term impact than underlying economic data.

This reflects a shift toward expectation-driven pricing. However, such a structure is inherently unstable. Expectations can be formed quickly, but they can also be reversed just as quickly when new information emerges or when previous assumptions are challenged.

When this happens, markets tend to reprice aggressively, as positions built on expectations unwind.

## The Illusion of Stability

Current market conditions may appear relatively stable, but this stability should be interpreted with caution.

The underlying drivers of volatility have not been resolved. Macro variables remain unsettled, geopolitical risks are still evolving, and policy paths are not yet fully defined.

In this context, price action is more likely to reflect temporary balance rather than a confirmed trend. Periods of consolidation should not be mistaken for structural stability.

Stability, in the current environment, is more accurately described as compressed volatility.

## Key Risk Factors

The primary risks facing the market are not isolated events, but the interaction between multiple variables.

A renewed escalation in geopolitical tensions could lead to a sharp repricing in energy markets. This could in turn impact broader financial conditions and risk appetite.

At the same time, shifts in rate expectations or liquidity conditions could reinforce these moves. When multiple factors align, market adjustments are likely to be faster and more pronounced.

The key risk lies in the potential for these variables to move simultaneously rather than independently.

## Conclusion

The market is currently in a transitional phase.

Liquidity is no longer the sole driver of price action. Macro conditions and geopolitical developments are increasingly shaping outcomes, and the interaction between these forces is creating a more complex and less predictable environment.

In such a setting, the focus should not be on short-term direction, but on structural understanding.

When key variables have not yet converged, trends are less likely to sustain. Markets may rally or stabilize in the short term, but this does not necessarily indicate that underlying risks have been resolved.

A more durable environment will require alignment across macro conditions and geopolitical developments. Until then, volatility should be expected, even during periods that appear stable on the surface.
