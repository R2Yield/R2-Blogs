## Markets Are Stabilizing, But Nothing Has Actually Been Resolved

## Introduction
If you only look at recent price action across major asset classes, it is easy to come away with the impression that markets are beginning to recover, as oil has pulled back from its recent highs, crypto has shown relative resilience, and equities have stopped extending their decline; however, this surface-level stabilization is somewhat misleading, because what we are actually seeing is not a meaningful improvement in underlying conditions, but rather a temporary easing of pressure after a period of elevated stress, where the system has found a short-term equilibrium without resolving any of the structural issues that caused the volatility in the first place.

In other words, markets are not pricing a return to strength, but instead are adjusting to a scenario where the worst-case outcomes have, at least for now, been pushed further out in time, and this shift from “immediate risk” to “delayed risk” is precisely what is allowing assets to stabilize.

### 1. From Escalation to Controlled Tension
What has changed most noticeably over the past week is not the existence of geopolitical tension, but the way in which it is being expressed, as both sides appear to be operating within a more constrained framework that avoids triggering systemic consequences, particularly in areas such as energy infrastructure, where escalation would have immediate and global economic repercussions.

This transition toward what can best be described as a “controlled conflict” does not imply that tensions are easing in any fundamental sense, but rather that there is a mutual recognition of certain boundaries that should not be crossed, and as long as those boundaries are respected, markets can begin to discount the probability of extreme tail events, even if the broader conflict remains unresolved.

The key point here is that markets are highly sensitive not only to the existence of risk, but to the shape and distribution of that risk, and a controlled environment, even if still unstable, is significantly easier to price than one where outcomes are unbounded.

### 2. Oil Pullback as a Function of Expectations, Not Fundamentals
The recent pullback in oil prices should not be interpreted as a signal that underlying supply-demand dynamics or geopolitical risks have fundamentally improved, but rather as a repricing of expectations following the realization that the most disruptive scenarios, such as widespread attacks on energy infrastructure or a full blockade of critical transit routes, are not materializing in the immediate term.

From a macro perspective, this distinction is important, because it implies that while short-term inflation expectations may ease as a result of lower energy prices, the structural drivers of inflation — including geopolitical fragmentation, fiscal expansion, and supply-side constraints — remain firmly in place, suggesting that any relief in inflation is likely to be temporary rather than permanent.

As a result, markets are currently benefiting from a near-term easing in pressure, while simultaneously underestimating the persistence of medium-term inflation risks.

### 3. Equities: Stabilization Without Structural Support
The behavior of equity markets over this period reflects a similar dynamic, where the absence of new negative catalysts has allowed prices to stabilize, but without any meaningful improvement in the underlying drivers of growth or liquidity, which remain constrained by elevated interest rates, tight financial conditions, and ongoing uncertainty around earnings.

In this context, the recent stabilization should not be interpreted as the beginning of a new upward trend, but rather as a pause within a broader process of repricing, where markets are adjusting to a higher cost of capital environment while still lacking a clear catalyst for sustained upside.

This creates a fragile equilibrium, where equities can hold or even grind higher in the short term, but remain vulnerable to renewed downside if macro conditions deteriorate again.

### 4. Crypto: Early Signs of Structural Repositioning
Against this backdrop, the relative strength observed in crypto markets is particularly notable, as it suggests that the asset class is beginning to decouple, at least partially, from traditional risk assets, and may be undergoing a transition in how it is perceived within the broader macro landscape.

While crypto has historically behaved as a high-beta extension of risk, its recent resilience in the face of equity weakness indicates that it is increasingly being positioned as a more nuanced asset, one that is sensitive to liquidity conditions but not entirely dependent on equity market direction, and that may benefit from structural narratives such as financial system diversification and alternative stores of value.

This does not imply that crypto is immune to macro shocks, but it does suggest that its role within portfolios is evolving, and that its price action is beginning to reflect flows that are not purely speculative.

### 5. Liquidity Remains the Core Constraint
Despite the short-term stabilization across multiple asset classes, the overarching constraint on markets remains unchanged, as global liquidity conditions continue to be shaped by restrictive monetary policy, elevated real yields, and limited scope for near-term easing, particularly in the absence of a significant deterioration in growth or financial conditions.

This means that while markets may continue to trade higher within a range, supported by positioning and temporary relief in macro pressures, the lack of expanding liquidity imposes a natural ceiling on risk-taking, making it difficult for any sustained or exponential rally to develop without a corresponding shift in policy.

In this sense, liquidity is not currently a catalyst, but a constraint, and until that changes, markets are likely to remain range-bound and reactive rather than trend-driven.

## Conclusion
The current market environment is best understood not as a recovery, but as a phase of temporary stabilization within a still fragile system, where risks have been deferred rather than resolved, and where the absence of immediate shocks has allowed for a partial repricing of risk without addressing the underlying structural pressures.

In practical terms, this suggests that markets can continue to hold or even move higher in the near term, but that such moves should be viewed within the context of a broader environment that remains constrained by geopolitical uncertainty, persistent inflation risks, and tight liquidity conditions.

For positioning, this is a regime that rewards flexibility over conviction, and structure over leverage, as the path forward is unlikely to be linear, and the balance between risk and opportunity remains highly dependent on how these macro variables evolve in the weeks ahead.
